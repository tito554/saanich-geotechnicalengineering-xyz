Much of Saanich sits on complex glacial stratigraphy that makes shallow foundation design anything but a copy-paste exercise. The advance and retreat of the Cordilleran ice sheet left behind a mosaic of lodgement till, glaciomarine silts, and outwash sands. What we see repeatedly in the field is that bearing capacity is rarely the limiting factor; the real challenge is controlling differential settlement where a footing bridges two distinct soil units. A well-calibrated design integrates detailed site investigation with a clear understanding of how these soils respond to seasonal moisture fluctuation. For sites where the upper crust is too variable, we often pair the design with a test pit investigation to physically log the contact between the weathered till and the underlying intact unit before finalizing the footing elevation.

On Saanich's glacial drifts, the allowable bearing pressure is dictated not by shear failure but by the settlement tolerance of the structure above.

Method and coverage

A recent project on a slope near Mount Douglas required careful evaluation of a spread footing for a two-storey addition. The site investigation revealed 1.8 m of loose colluvium overlying dense Vashon till. The design had to satisfy two competing demands: keep the excavation shallow enough to avoid undermining the adjacent heritage trees, yet deep enough to bypass the compressible surface layer. The solution involved a stepped strip footing with a 600 mm key into the till, combined with a rigorous settlement analysis under the NBCC serviceability limit state. This scenario is typical of many Saanich slopes facing the Haro Strait, where shallow foundations must also account for the long-term stability of the cut. In these cases, a reliable slope stability assessment feeds directly into the foundation geometry and drainage detailing.

Regional considerations

Saanich sits within a region of moderate to high seismic hazard; the Leech River and Devil's Mountain fault zones are active geological structures that influence the design spectrum. For shallow foundations on loose saturated sands or silts, cyclic mobility and liquefaction become design-critical. A footing that performs acceptably under static load can experience a bearing capacity failure or excessive settlement if the supporting soil loses strength during a design-level earthquake. The NBCC 2020 requires site-specific seismic hazard analysis for Site Classes D and E, which cover the lowland marine deposits around the Saanich Inlet and the Cordova Bay lowlands. Another common risk we address is the presence of soft, compressible lenses within the Quadra Sand unit; these can produce localized settlement that goes unnoticed until the structure is framed, and then becomes expensive to remediate.

Quick answers

How much does a shallow foundation design cost for a residential project in Saanich?

For a single-family home or a small addition in Saanich, the engineering design fee typically falls between CA$2,360 and CA$4,530. The final amount depends on the complexity of the soil profile, the number of footing elements, and whether supplementary investigation like test pits or laboratory testing is required.

Which soil types in Saanich are most problematic for shallow foundations?

The glaciomarine silts and clays found in the low-lying areas near Cordova Bay and the Patricia Bay flats present the most challenges. These soils can be normally consolidated or lightly overconsolidated, leading to significant consolidation settlement under load. Additionally, loose deposits of the Quadra Sand unit are prone to seismic densification and require careful evaluation.

Do I always need a deep foundation if the soil is soft near the surface?

Not necessarily. In many Saanich sites, the soft surface layer is relatively thin and can be excavated and replaced with engineered structural fill. A shallow footing can then bear on the compacted fill or the stiffer underlying unit. We evaluate this on a case-by-case basis by comparing the cost of over-excavation against the cost of switching to a deep foundation system.
