Saanich’s growth from an agricultural hub to a core district of the Capital Regional District placed increasing demands on glacial stratigraphy mapping, where the Vashon till and glaciomarine silts can hide abrupt bedrock relief. We run seismic tomography campaigns—both P-wave refraction and high-resolution reflection—that give engineers a continuous velocity cross-section rather than single-point borehole data, which is especially helpful where the Cordova Bay gravels transition into the Colwood drift complex. For deeper objectives near the Saanich Inlet, combining refraction with MASW surveys helps distinguish weathered from competent bedrock, while seismic refraction profiling alone often suffices on the till-mantled slopes of the Goward Road area.

A seismic tomogram reveals bedrock topography that a grid of test pits cannot resolve—critical when bearing strata vary by metres across a single building footprint.

Method and coverage

The contrast between the low-lying Blenkinsop Valley and the rock-controlled terrain around Mount Douglas illustrates why seismic tomography parameters must be tuned to each site. In the valley, saturated organic silts overlying Pleistocene clays produce strong velocity gradients that demand dense geophone spacing and dual-source impacts, whereas the granodiorite outcrops near Mount Douglas require high-energy sledgehammer or weight-drop sources to achieve sufficient ray coverage at depth. Our crew digitizes the seismograms on site, runs iterative ray-tracing inversion, and delivers tomograms that map the bedrock surface within half a metre, which allows foundation designers to position deep excavation supports precisely where refusal depth changes rapidly.

Regional considerations

A recent three-tower residential project on McKenzie Avenue encountered a buried paleochannel filled with loose organic silt beneath a stiff till cap. Standard borehole interpolation missed the channel edges by nearly fifteen metres, which would have placed pile tips inside compressible material. We deployed a 96-metre refraction line with 2-metre geophone spacing and ran a reflection walkaway survey over the suspect zone. The resulting velocity tomogram mapped the trough boundaries to within two metres, allowing the geotechnical engineer to adjust pile lengths and avoid differential settlement across the podium. Omitting seismic tomography on glacially disturbed sites in Saanich means accepting interpolation risk that can translate into costly foundation remediation after excavation reveals the real profile.

Quick answers

What depth can seismic refraction tomography reach in Saanich glacial deposits?

With a 115-metre spread and an accelerated weight drop, we typically image to 30–40 metres in till and outwash. In competent granodiorite near Mount Douglas, depth of investigation can exceed 50 metres when low-frequency geophones are used.

How much does a seismic tomography survey cost in the Saanich area?

Most Saanich projects fall between CA$3,510 for a short single-line refraction profile and CA$7,580 for a multi-line grid with combined refraction and MASW acquisition. The final figure depends on line length, channel count, and site access.

Can seismic tomography distinguish between weathered and intact bedrock?

Yes. The velocity gradient from weathered bedrock (typically 1,200–2,000 m/s) to intact granodiorite (4,500–5,500 m/s) produces a sharp tomographic contrast that we interpret as the engineering bedrock surface.

How soon are results available after field acquisition?

We deliver preliminary tomograms and an interpreted bedrock contour within three business days. The final signed report, including Vs30 site classification and NBCC commentary, follows within five working days.
