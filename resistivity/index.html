Saanich’s development pattern—from the Cordova Bay shoreline to the rocky uplands near Mount Douglas—has always been dictated by the underlying geology, a complex mix of glacial till, marine clays, and fractured basalt bedrock left by the Cordilleran ice sheet. Subsurface exploration here requires non-invasive methods that can distinguish between water-saturated silts and competent rock without disturbing the ground. Electrical resistivity surveying, particularly the vertical electrical sounding (VES) technique, resolves these contrasts by measuring how formations resist current flow, which is directly tied to grain size, water content, and salinity. The team applies ASTM D6431 procedures with multi-electrode arrays to deliver stratigraphic models for foundation design, slope stability assessments, and groundwater investigations across the municipality. For deeper investigations in the Elk Lake basin, where soft sediments extend beyond 30 meters, we often pair the VES data with a seismic refraction survey to cross-validate bedrock depth and rippability.

A resistivity contrast of 10:1 between saturated marine clay and dry glacial till can shift the interpreted bedrock depth by several meters if not calibrated against local borehole stratigraphy.

Method and coverage

The strong precipitation gradient across the Saanich Peninsula—from the wetter western slopes receiving over 1,000 mm of annual rainfall to the drier rain-shadow areas near Haro Strait—creates significant variability in subsurface moisture that directly impacts resistivity readings. A VES sounding performed in the saturated clays of the Blenkinsop Valley will yield resistivity values below 20 ohm-m, whereas the same glacial till on a well-drained slope near Prospect Lake may register above 100 ohm-m. This sensitivity makes the method invaluable for mapping perched water tables, saltwater intrusion in coastal zones, and contaminant plumes. Our resistivity equipment employs a Wenner-Schlumberger array configuration with automated stacking to suppress cultural noise from Saanich’s buried utilities and transit corridors. Interpretation involves 1D inversion with smooth-model constraints calibrated against available borehole logs, and when site conditions warrant a broader lateral profile, we integrate the VES transects with MASW testing to build a combined geoelectric-seismic cross-section that reveals both stiffness and saturation trends.

Regional considerations

Comparing two sites—one on a rocky promontory in the Gordon Head neighbourhood and another on the low-lying agricultural soils of the Blenkinsop Valley—highlights the stark contrasts that a resistivity survey must resolve. At Gordon Head, a thin veneer of Vashon till overlying granodiorite creates a steep resistivity gradient near surface, where misidentifying a shallow boulder bed as bedrock can lead to excavation delays and change orders. In the valley, thick sequences of glaciomarine clay and peat exhibit resistivities below 10 ohm-m, and the risk shifts to undetected pockets of organic material or artesian groundwater that destabilize footings long after construction. Skipping a dedicated geophysical survey in Saanich means relying on interpolation between sparse boreholes, which often misses lateral changes in the Queen Charlotte sediments that define bearing capacity. The resulting structural distress—differential settlement, retaining wall rotation, or basement water ingress—typically manifests within the first five years and costs far more than the initial investigation.

Other technical services

01

Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES) Surveys

Point-depth resistivity profiling using expanding Wenner or Schlumberger arrays to map layer thickness, bedrock elevation, and groundwater zones. Each sounding is inverted against regional borehole data to produce a geoelectric log with interpreted lithology.

02

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT)

Multi-electrode profiling along linear transects for cross-sectional imaging of lateral discontinuities, fault zones, and contaminant boundaries. Ideal for slope stability corridors and pipeline right-of-ways in the Saanich Highlands.

03

Soil Corrosivity Assessment

On-site resistivity testing per ASTM G57 to classify soil aggressiveness toward buried steel and concrete. Critical for infrastructure projects along the Pat Bay Highway corridor where saline intrusion accelerates corrosion rates.

Quick answers

How much does a VES resistivity survey cost for a typical residential lot in Saanich?

For a standard residential parcel, a VES survey with three soundings and an interpreted report typically ranges from CA$770 to CA$1,570, depending on access, terrain, and the depth of investigation required. Steep wooded sites or properties with extensive surface paving may require additional setup time that adjusts the final figure.

What depth can a VES sounding reach in the Saanich glacial deposits?

The maximum depth depends on the maximum current electrode spacing (AB/2) and the subsurface resistivity. In conductive glaciomarine clays of the Blenkinsop Valley, penetration is limited to roughly 25 to 35 meters with practical cable lengths, whereas in the resistive till and bedrock terrain of the Highlands, we routinely profile to 50 meters or more using a 200-meter spread.

How do you calibrate resistivity data without drilling in Saanich?

Absolute calibration always benefits from at least one borehole or test pit, but where none exists, we tie our inversion models to published stratigraphic logs from the Geological Survey of Canada’s Victoria map sheet and the Capital Regional District’s well records. The resistivity of the Vashon till and Quadra Sand units in Saanich is well-characterized, providing reliable starting models for 1D inversion.

Can resistivity distinguish between saltwater intrusion and natural saline clays near Cordova Bay?

Both produce very low resistivity (<5 ohm-m), but time-lapse resistivity monitoring combined with chloride sampling from shallow piezometers can differentiate a moving freshwater-saltwater interface from static low-resistivity clay formations. We recommend a paired resistivity and groundwater sampling program for coastal properties within 500 meters of the shoreline.
